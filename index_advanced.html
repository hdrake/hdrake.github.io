Current Research

I am currently a research assistant in the department of Atmospheric and Oceanic Sciences (AOS) at Princeton University, where I work on Southern Ocean upwelling pathways in Professor Jorge Sarmiento's Biogeochemistry group. Under the mentorship of Princeton University postdoc Adele Morrison, Princeton University postdoc Alison Gray and Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory (GFDL) physical scientist Steve Griffies, I use Lagrangian and Eulerian diagnostics to uncover the three-dimensional structure of Southern Ocean deep water upwelling pathways using both models (GFDL's CM2 suite of Ocean-Atmosphere General Circulation Models) and observations (Argo floats). I am particularly interested in the role of model resolution, mesoscale eddies, and topographical asymmetries on these upwelling pathways and methods for diagnosing their effects.

Other Academic Interests

Personal and Academic Background

I grew up in San Francisco, where I attending the French American International School (k-12). After graduating from high school, I moved to the East Coast for my undergraduate studies at Haverford College near Philadelphia. There, I focused on Physics and Mathematics, dabbling here and there in the few Environmental Science and Scientific Computing courses that seemed relevant to my interests in climate physics. During my three undergraduate summer breaks, I did research in an Environmental Engineering / Remote Sensing laboratory under Professor James Bonner at Clarkson University, in a Quantum Computing laboratory under Professor Daniel Lidar at the University of Southern California, and in the mathematical theory of knots under Professor Joshua Sabloff at Haverford College. For my senior thesis in mathematics at Haverford College, I worked on rigorously proving an algorithm for computing geodesic paths in phylogenetic tree space (with applications to phylogenetics and lung disease diagnostics) and was advised by Professor Elizabeth Townsend Milicevic.
